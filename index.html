Circulating Tumor Cell Imaging

Wong Bioimaging Engineering Laboratory Brown University



Abstract

This project investigated how extracellular matrix stiffness influences circulating tumor cell (CTC) morphology and behavior during cancer progression. Circulating tumor cells are cancer cells that detach from primary tumors and travel through the bloodstream, where they may eventually seed metastatic tumors in distant tissues. To better understand how physical microenvironments shape cellular structure and invasive potential, CTCs were cultured on soft and stiff polyacrylamide gel substrates designed to simulate tissue-like environments. By combining fluorescence microscopy with computational image segmentation and morphology analysis, this work explored how substrate stiffness alters cellular spreading, elongation, and structural organization.



Research Approach

  • Circulating tumor cells were cultured on soft (0.5 kPa) and stiff (5 kPa) polyacrylamide gel substrates over a three-month experimental period to simulate distinct extracellular microenvironments. To visualize cellular structure, cytoplasmic morphology was labeled with CellMask (Cy5 channel, red), while nuclear morphology was captured with Hoechst (DAPI channel, blue). High-resolution fluorescence microscopy imaging captured these structural adaptations, highlighting visible morphology differences between oft and stiff environments. Cells cultured on stiffer substrates demonstrated increased spreading and structural irregularity, exhibiting more elongated and blob-like morphologies compared to the rounder and more compact cellular organization observed in softer extracellular environments.

    • Fluorescence microscopy imaging of CTCs

    • We quantified these visual differences by applying principal component analysis (PCA) to fluorescence microscopy imaging datasets in Excel. PCA results were visualized using heatmaps, where deeper red values indicate stronger positive contributions to principal component variation and deeper blue values indicate stronger negative contributions. These heatmaps simplified the interpretation of complex morphology datasets and highlighted structural features most strongly associated with extracellular matrix stiffness. Our analysis focused primarily on features that effectively captured differences in cellular spreading, elongation, and structural integrity between microenvironments: area, ellipse, major axis length, and compactness.

    The strongest morphology trends across both Cy5 and DAPI datasets were associated with major axis length and compactness, suggesting that extracellular stiffness strongly influences cellular elongation and structural organization.

    Major axis length demonstrated strong positive loading shifts across both channels, increasing from 0.22 to 0.29 in Cy5 and from -0.23 to 0.29 in DAPI, indicating increased cellular elongation under stiff extracellular conditions. In contrast, compactness demonstrated consistently negative loading behavior, ranging from -0.29 to 0.18 in Cy5 and remaining strongly negative in DAPI (-0.21 to -0.28), suggesting that cells cultured on stiff substrates adopted less circular and more structurally irregular morphologies.

    Area and ellipse area also demonstrated substantial loading variation. Cy5 datasets maintained strong positive loading values ranging from 0.22–0.28, while DAPI datasets shifted from strong negative values (-0.28 and -0.27) to positive PC2 contributions (0.22 and 0.23). These trends suggest that substrate stiffness influences cytoplasmic spreading and nuclear structural organization differently, while still producing coordinated morphology adaptation across the cell.

    Notably, Cy5 morphology features generally demonstrated stronger positive spreading-related loadings, while DAPI datasets exhibited larger negative-to-positive transitions across several features. This suggests that cytoplasmic remodeling may occur more gradually through increased spreading and elongation, whereas nuclear morphology undergoes more pronounced structural reorganization in response to extracellular stiffness. Across nearly all major features, stiff extracellular environments consistently shifted cells toward elongated, less compact, and more structurally irregular morphologies.

    Together, these results indicate that cells cultured on stiff polyacrylamide gels exhibit increased spreading, elongation, and morphological irregularity compared to cells grown on softer extracellular environments. These structural adaptations are commonly associated with cytoskeletal remodeling, increased migratory behavior, and invasive metastatic potential, supporting the conclusion that extracellular matrix stiffness plays a significant role in shaping circulating tumor cell behavior during cancer progression.
