Circulating Tumor Cell Imaging

Wong Bioimaging Engineering Laboratory Brown University



Abstract

This project investigated how extracellular matrix stiffness influences circulating tumor cell (CTC) morphology and behavior during cancer progression. Circulating tumor cells are cancer cells that detach from primary tumors and travel through the bloodstream, where they may eventually seed metastatic tumors in distant tissues. To better understand how physical microenvironments shape cellular structure and invasive potential, CTCs were cultured on soft and stiff polyacrylamide gel substrates designed to simulate tissue-like environments. By combining fluorescence microscopy with computational image segmentation and morphology analysis, this work explored how substrate stiffness alters cellular spreading, elongation, and structural organization.



Research Approach

    Circulating tumor cells were cultured on soft (0.5 kPa) and stiff (5 kPa) polyacrylamide gel substrates over a three-month experimental period to simulate distinct extracellular microenvironments. To visualize cellular structure, cytoplasmic morphology was labeled with CellMask (Cy5 channel, red), while nuclear morphology was captured with Hoechst (DAPI channel, blue). High-resolution fluorescence microscopy imaging captured these structural adaptations, highlighting visible morphology differences between oft and stiff environments. Cells cultured on stiffer substrates demonstrated increased spreading and structural irregularity, exhibiting more elongated and blob-like morphologies compared to the rounder and more compact cellular organization observed in softer extracellular environments.

    Fluorescence microscopy imaging of CTCs

      We quantified these visual differences by applying principal component analysis (PCA) to fluorescence microscopy imaging datasets in Excel. PCA results were visualized using heatmaps, where deeper red values indicate stronger positive contributions to principal component variation and deeper blue values indicate stronger negative contributions. These heatmaps simplified the interpretation of complex morphology datasets and highlighted structural features most strongly associated with extracellular matrix stiffness. Our analysis focused primarily on features that effectively captured differences in cellular spreading, elongation, and structural integrity between microenvironments: area, ellipse, major axis length, and compactness.

    The strongest morphology trends across both Cy5 and DAPI datasets were associated with major axis length and compactness, suggesting that extracellular stiffness strongly influences cellular elongation and structural organization.

    Major axis length demonstrated strong positive loading shifts across both channels, increasing from 0.22 to 0.29 in Cy5 and from -0.23 to 0.29 in DAPI, indicating increased cellular elongation under stiff extracellular conditions. In contrast, compactness demonstrated consistently negative loading behavior, ranging from -0.29 to 0.18 in Cy5 and remaining strongly negative in DAPI (-0.21 to -0.28), suggesting that cells cultured on stiff substrates adopted less circular and more structurally irregular morphologies.

    Area and ellipse area also demonstrated substantial loading variation. Cy5 datasets maintained strong positive loading values ranging from 0.22–0.28, while DAPI datasets shifted from strong negative values (-0.28 and -0.27) to positive PC2 contributions (0.22 and 0.23). These trends suggest that substrate stiffness influences cytoplasmic spreading and nuclear structural organization differently, while still producing coordinated morphology adaptation across the cell.

    Notably, Cy5 morphology features generally demonstrated stronger positive spreading-related loadings, while DAPI datasets exhibited larger negative-to-positive transitions across several features. This suggests that cytoplasmic remodeling may occur more gradually through increased spreading and elongation, whereas nuclear morphology undergoes more pronounced structural reorganization in response to extracellular stiffness. Across nearly all major features, stiff extracellular environments consistently shifted cells toward elongated, less compact, and more structurally irregular morphologies.

    Together, these results indicate that cells cultured on stiff polyacrylamide gels exhibit increased spreading, elongation, and morphological irregularity compared to cells grown on softer extracellular environments. These structural adaptations are commonly associated with cytoskeletal remodeling, increased migratory behavior, and invasive metastatic potential, supporting the conclusion that extracellular matrix stiffness plays a significant role in shaping circulating tumor cell behavior during cancer progression.

HipHarmony

Engineering Design Project | Orthopedic Implant Design



Overview

HipHarmoney is an athlete-focused hip resurfacing implant designed to improve durability, mobility, and long-term performance for young and highly active patients. Unlike traditional hip replacements, which are primarily optimized for older adults, HipHarmony prioritizes bone preservation, reduced wear, and a faster return to activity. Through user-centered design, biomaterials research, CAD modeling, prototyping, and finite element analysis (FEA), our team developed a concept combining ceramic articulation surfaces, titanium structural support, and porous fixation technology to create a durable solution for high-performance users.

Human-Centered Design

To guide the development of HipHarmony, we created multiple user personas and evaluated several target populations, including retirees, labor-intensive workers, recreational athletes, and professional athletes. Through persona mapping, empathy mapping, and journey mapping, we identified professional athletes as the population that would benefit most from a bone-preserving, high-performance hip resurfacing implant. Our primary persona, Nathan, is a 30-year-old professional soccer player experiencing severe hip pain that threatens both his athletic career and quality of life. Nathan values bone preservation, long-term mobility, rapid recovery, and the ability to continue competing at an elite level. This user-centered approach shaped every major engineering decision throughout the project.

Empathy map highlighting the user's goals, concerns, behaviors, and emotions.

Journey map illustrating the patient experience from injury through recovery.

Based on the insights gathered from Nathan's persona, empathy map, and journey map, we established the following engineering design requirements for HipHarmony:

  • Durability: Withstand repetitive, high-impact athletic activity.
  • Mobility: Preserve natural range of motion and enable return to sport.
  • Bone Preservation: Maintain healthy anatomy and preserve future treatment options.
  • Stability: Remain secure during dynamic movements such as running and jumping.
  • Recovery: Support faster rehabilitation and return to daily activities.
  • Longevity: Minimize the likelihood of revision surgeries.
  • Biocompatibility: Promote patient safety while minimizing wear and complications.

Prototyping

Early Ideation Prototypes

Based on these early ideation concepts, we refined the overall implant architecture and selected the following structural components for the final HipHarmony design.

  • Zirconia-Toughened Alumina (ZTA) Ceramic was selected for the articulation surface because of its exceptional wear resistance, low friction, and long-term durability compared to traditional metal-on-metal systems.
  • Titanium Acetabular Cup provides a lightweight yet mechanically strong structural foundation while maintaining excellent biocompatibility and corrosion resistance.
  • Porous Surface Coating promotes bone ingrowth, strengthening biological fixation and improving long-term implant stability.
  • Uncemented Fixation allows the implant to naturally integrate with surrounding bone while reducing dependence on bone cement.
  • Titanium Alloy Screws provide additional fixation during early recovery and improve stability under high-impact loading.
  • Bone-Preserving Hip Resurfacing retains much of the patient's natural femoral head, preserving healthy bone and making future revision procedures easier if needed.

Finite Element Analysis (FEA)

To evaluate the structural integrity of the final design, we performed Finite Element Analysis (FEA) in Fusion 360 under a simulated 600 N (130 lb) loading condition. The simulation was used to examine stress and strain distributions throughout the implant and identify areas that may require future optimization while verifying that the design remained structurally stable.

Von Mises stress distribution under a simulated 600 N loading condition.

Strain distribution across the implant during structural simulation.

Simulation Results

The simulation demonstrated that HipHarmony maintained structural stability under the applied loading condition. Stress was concentrated primarily around fixation interfaces and load-bearing regions, while strain remained within expected ranges across the implant. These results suggest that the ceramic articulation surfaces, titanium structural components, and porous fixation strategy effectively distribute mechanical loads while supporting long-term durability and stability.

  • Validated structural performance using a 600 N loading simulation.
  • Identified regions of highest stress concentration for future optimization.
  • Confirmed stable stress and strain distribution across the implant.
  • Demonstrated how computational simulation can guide medical device design before manufacturing.



Design Evaluation & Future Improvements

HipHarmony successfully met many of the original design objectives by prioritizing durability, bone preservation, mobility, and long-term stability for highly active patients. While the design demonstrates strong engineering potential, several opportunities remain for further optimization before clinical implementation.
